Let's Talk
ID Cheat Sheet

Every ID framework,
clearly explained.

A practical reference for the ID models I apply in real projects. Not theory for theory's sake — each framework explained as a design decision, with when to use it and when not to.

ADDIE
Analysis · Design · Development · Implementation · Evaluation

The industry-standard instructional design framework. A five-phase iterative process that provides structure without sacrificing flexibility. Best for projects with clear scope, defined SMEs, and stakeholders who need a documented process.

Formal corporate eLearning, regulated industries, large-scale deployments
Rapidly changing content, agile environments, short timelines
Anti-Money Laundering, Code of Conduct, World Vision India
A
Analysis
Identify the performance gap, audience characteristics, learning environment, and constraints. The most important phase — most design failures start with skipping it.
D
Design
Define learning objectives (using Bloom's verbs), assessment strategy, sequence, and media approach. This phase produces the blueprint the development team builds from.
D
Development
Build the actual course — storyboards, interactions, media assets, SCORM packaging. Iterative review cycles with SMEs happen here.
I
Implementation
Deploy to LMS, train facilitators, communicate to learners. This phase is underplanned by most teams and over-relied upon when the earlier phases were weak.
E
Evaluation
Measure against objectives. Did learners gain the intended knowledge? Did behaviour change on the job? Kirkpatrick's model is the companion framework here.
Bloom's
Bloom's Taxonomy of Educational Objectives

The framework for writing learning objectives that actually mean something. Six cognitive levels from Remember to Create — each requiring fundamentally different instructional and assessment approaches. Used in every single course design decision.

Writing learning objectives, designing assessments, aligning content to cognitive level
Most corporate training targets Remember. Most job performance requires Apply or Analyse.
1
Remember
Recall facts and basic concepts. Verbs: define, list, recall, identify. Assessment: multiple choice, matching. Often over-used in compliance training.
2
Understand
Explain ideas or concepts. Verbs: describe, explain, summarise, interpret. Assessment: short answer, concept mapping.
3
Apply
Use information in new situations. Verbs: use, demonstrate, execute, solve. Assessment: scenarios, simulations, case studies. This is where learning becomes useful.
4
Analyse
Draw connections, break down information. Verbs: differentiate, organise, compare. Assessment: branching scenarios, case analysis.
5
Evaluate
Justify a decision or course of action. Verbs: judge, critique, defend, recommend. Assessment: reflection prompts, structured debates.
6
Create
Produce new or original work. Verbs: design, construct, develop, formulate. Assessment: projects, portfolios, performance tasks. The highest cognitive demand.
SAM
Successive Approximation Model

Michael Allen's agile alternative to ADDIE. Rather than completing one phase before starting the next, SAM uses rapid prototyping and iterative design cycles. The best model when timelines are tight, content evolves, or stakeholder feedback is hard to get upfront.

Agile environments, SMEs who give better feedback on prototypes than documents, fast-turnaround projects
Failures happen early and cheaply. You discover what doesn't work before full development.
1
Preparation Phase
Background research and initial information gathering. Brief — not exhaustive. The goal is enough to prototype, not enough to over-specify.
2
Iterative Design
Rapid prototyping cycles: design → prototype → review → repeat. Stakeholders react to something tangible, not a document. This closes the feedback gap that plagues waterfall projects.
3
Iterative Development
Design, develop, and implement in overlapping cycles. Alpha → Beta → Gold master. Each cycle produces a working, reviewable asset.

When I Use SAM

  • Client can't articulate requirements until they see something
  • Content changes frequently during development
  • Timeline doesn't allow sequential ADDIE phases
  • SMEs are more reactive than proactive reviewers
Gagné
Gagné's Nine Events of Instruction

Robert Gagné's nine-step sequence based on cognitive processing theory. Each event maps to a specific internal learning condition. The most useful framework for structuring a single lesson or module — especially for procedural or concept-based learning.

Single-lesson structure, procedural training, onboarding modules
The nine events are not a rigid script — they're cognitive checkpoints to ensure nothing is missing.
1
Gain Attention
Hook the learner. A startling statistic, a provocative question, a video scenario. If you lose them here, the rest of the course is wasted.
2
State the Objective
Tell learners exactly what they'll be able to do. Clarity removes cognitive load spent wondering "why am I doing this?"
3
Stimulate Recall of Prior Learning
Connect new content to what learners already know. New knowledge is anchored more effectively when tied to existing schema.
4
Present the Content
Deliver the new knowledge or skill — chunked, sequenced, and scaffolded to avoid cognitive overload.
5
Provide Learning Guidance
Worked examples, analogies, job aids. Show learners how to use the new knowledge before asking them to do it alone.
6
Elicit Performance
Practice. The learner applies the skill — in a scenario, simulation, or exercise. No practice = no learning.
7
Provide Feedback
Immediate, specific, corrective feedback. Not "incorrect, try again" — but why it's wrong and what to do differently.
8
Assess Performance
A formal check: can the learner do this independently? The assessment must match the objective — same cognitive level, same context.
9
Enhance Retention & Transfer
Spaced practice, job aids, follow-up activities. Learning degrades without reinforcement. This step is consistently the most skipped.
Merrill
Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

David Merrill's five principles derived from reviewing the best instructional design models. Less prescriptive than Gagné, more outcome-focused. The key insight: learning is promoted when instruction is centred on real-world problems, not isolated content coverage.

Complex skill development, problem-solving training, performance-centred design
Learning is facilitated when learners are engaged in solving real-world problems.
1
Problem-Centred
Learning is promoted when learners are engaged in solving a real-world problem. Not "here is the knowledge" — but "here is the problem you need to solve with this knowledge."
2
Activation
Learning is promoted when existing knowledge is activated as a foundation for new knowledge. Relevance bridges prior experience to new content.
3
Demonstration
Learning is promoted when new knowledge is demonstrated, not just told. Show the skill being performed in context, not just described abstractly.
4
Application
Learning is promoted when learners apply new knowledge and skills. Guided practice progressing to independent practice. Feedback is essential.
5
Integration
Learning is promoted when learners integrate new knowledge into their real-world context. Reflection, discussion, or immediate on-the-job application consolidates the learning.
Kirk-
patrick
Kirkpatrick's Four Levels of Training Evaluation

The most widely used evaluation framework in L&D. Four levels from learner reaction to business results. The critical insight most teams miss: you should design evaluation from Level 4 backwards, not measure from Level 1 forwards.

Demonstrating L&D value to stakeholders, measurement strategy, ROI conversations
Most organisations measure Level 1. Almost none measure Level 3 or 4. That's where value lives.
1
Reaction
Did learners like it? Smile sheets, satisfaction surveys. The most measured level — and the least useful predictor of learning or performance change. Necessary but insufficient.
2
Learning
Did learners gain knowledge, skills, or attitude change? Pre/post assessments, knowledge checks, skills demonstrations. The level most course designers focus on.
3
Behaviour
Did learners apply new knowledge on the job? Manager observations, 360 feedback, performance monitoring. Rarely measured — the biggest gap in most L&D programmes.
4
Results
Did the training produce the intended business outcome? Error reduction, sales increase, compliance rate, safety incidents. This is the conversation executives actually care about.
Action
Mapping
Cathy Moore's Action Mapping

Cathy Moore's approach that starts by asking "what do people need to DO?" rather than "what do they need to KNOW?" Ruthlessly strips out information-delivery in favour of practice activities. The best antidote to content-heavy, click-next eLearning.

Any project where the client's instinct is to dump information into slides
Completed Cathy Moore's official Action Mapping certification — the framework I reach for first on every new project.
1
Identify the Business Goal
What measurable result is the organisation trying to achieve? Not "we need a course on X" but "we need to reduce errors / increase sales / reduce incidents by Y%."
2
Identify Actions That Drive the Goal
What specific behaviours need to change on the job? Map the actions people need to do differently, not the knowledge they need to possess.
3
Design Practice Activities
For each action, design a realistic practice scenario — not a knowledge check. Learners should practise doing, not recalling.
4
Identify Minimum Necessary Information
Add only the information learners need to complete the practice activities. Everything else is noise. This step eliminates 40-60% of most clients' initial content lists.
